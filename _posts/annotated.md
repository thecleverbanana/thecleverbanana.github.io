## Let's be honest about Australia and ditch the stereotypes. Will the real Australians please stand up?
### Richard Glover
Mark Twain said 


Australian history was full of "the most beautiful lies" – but it's nothing compared to the lies we tell each other every day. 
Almost all the stereotypes about Australia – the things we actively promote and believe to be true – are the antithesis of what really goes on. 
This Australia Day could we, just for a moment, be honest about who we are? We might even discover that sometimes the 
truth is better than the stereotype.

·       
We love to think of ourselves as 
larrikins, but 
we rather 
enjoy rules – and lots of them. The populist response to almost any problem is to demand new laws; the role of government has been to resist a populace constantly offering its own arms for various 
straitjackets. 
Hardly any other nation tells cyclists they must wear helmets; most places don't issue a ticket if you park facing the wrong way on a suburban street. 
Our 
collectivist ethos – and this isn't always a bad thing – invariably trumps our 
rebellious spirit.
 
 
·      
 
 
Nearly all our 
wildlife is cute and 
cuddly, yet we've convinced ourselves and the rest of the world that 
we live in the most dangerous place on earth. In our own minds, we are 
have-a-go-heroes for just walking out the front door. Crocodile and shark attacks are rare, but reporting them to the world has
 become 
a major Australian export industry. And, sure, we have all 10 of the world's most dangerous snakes, but all 10 live beneath the same rock in the Great Sandy Desert. In the end we've had to create mythical beasts – Drop Bears, Bunyips and Yowies – just to feed the national hunger for animal-based horror.
 
 
·     
  We think of ourselves, particularly our men, as 
laconic and good at 
hiding their emotions. In fact, we half-suspect 
they haven't got any. 
In reality, 
Australian men cry almost 
constantly. We are wonderfully emotionally 
labile. Our prime ministers, in particular, take the merest breaks from almost constant 
lachrymosity. Maybe it's the dust. You'll have to excuse me right now, there's a tiny speck in my eye.
 
 
·  
     
We think of 
Australian women as down-trodden door mats – still suffering from the male-dominated society created by transportation and then 
reinforced by the men who joined the gold rush of the 1850s. 
It's a good theory, 
but here's my question: 
have you actually met any Australian women?
 
 
·       We believe we speak English, 
but it's a 
very particular form of English. You try making yourself understood elsewhere 
when you observe that "Shazza and Mazza played Acca-Dacca outside 
Maccas." And it's not only the constant abbreviations. 
Some Australian vocabulary is just plain weird. 
Most English-speakers would leave the house "barefoot". Only Australians leave the house "wearing bare feet". 
Really? At what point do we slip them on?
 
 
·      
 
Per head, we're 
among the three richest nations in the world, but we're the only place in which politicians, keen to suck up, routinely describe the middle class as "battlers". Maybe we could re-write 
The 
Communist Manifesto: "
Battlers of Australia, arise, you have nothing to lose but your house, wide-screen TV and three-car-garage."
 
 
·      
 We've gained more from 
globalisation than most places, but still lurch into the mentality of fortress Australia. I blame the person who designed the first map of the world and decided to make
 south the bottom. Every time an Australian looks at a map, there's a moment of terror: surely mere gravity will bring all of Asia to our shores.
 
 
·       
We think of ourselves as a sunny, up-beat people, but all our heroes are losers. We hanker for stories that end in disaster – Phar Lap, the ANZACs at Gallipoli, Les Darcy, Burke and Wills, Ludwig Leichhardt, Charles O'Connor. At the start of every history lesson at primary school, you just knew all the protagonists in the story were about to die. Our economists, too, have good form: they've predicted 13 of the last two recessions. If we ever want a national slogan, it should be: "This isn't going to end well."
 
 
·       We think we're doggedly loyal, but as a nation we can be quite tough on our friends. When Australians talk about World War I, you'd think the enemy was Britain – and that, in the battle against them, we lost. During World War II, by contrast, the enemy was still Britain, but this time we won. We now sound off about  America in the same way. Of course, we'd never complain so vigorously if we thought there was a real chance the Americans would wave us goodbye.
 
 
·       
We think our history is boring, when it's the opposite. It's the triumphant story of the world's oldest continuing civilisation, and the darker, but sometimes inspiring, story of what happened next. It's also the tale of one of the world's most remarkable social experiments: the decision to take some of Britain's most impoverished people, dump them on an island, and see if they thrived.
They did. We did. Then we became adept at telling the most beautiful lies.
Whatever it means to be Australian, Happy 
Australia Day.
